Subsonic Wind Tunnel

(May 2024 - Present)

Introduction

I have always wanted to make a subsonic wind tunnel due to two main reasons: (1) Visualize streamlines over various types of airfoils and (2) Measure lift and drag force over test articles and compare with NACA charts.

Design

Minimizing build cost and ensuring ease of transportation were the two main goals. I started by choosing the inlet's contraction ratio, as this would drive the size of the test section and diffuser. I chose a 5:1 contraction ratio for maximum airspeed inside the test section, while keeping the inlet dimensions feasible to manufacture and transport.

Inlet: Initially, I designed a cone-shaped inlet, but then changed to a polynomial-curve shape for a better aesthetic. I chose to join the test section to the inlet and diffuser with hinges to allow for disassembly for transportation. To ensure laminar airflow, I plan to use a wireframe mesh to break down turbulent eddies in the intake air.

Sting arm: A sting arm protruding inside the test section both holds the test article, and changes its angle of attack (through a servo). To read lift and drag forces, I will etch strain gages (in a half-bridge configuration) and read the voltage using a custom PCB (for amplifying the input voltage) and an Arduino. This voltage will then be converted to a strain, from where I can obtain the forces.

Manufacturing and Assembly

I used a CNC Gantry router (at Purdueâ€™s BIDC ) to machine the inlet, test section and diffuser from plywood. Then, I assembled the main sections using two part structural epoxy and wood screws.

For the inlet contraction cone, I used a laser cutter to cut the shapes out of cardboard sheets. When assembling the sheets together, I realized I should have provided some relief length for the sheet to relax after being bent into a contour. Additionally, there was a gap between the sheets because they were all the same shape. I plan to make a 2nd iteration of the cone, using a flat pattern (from CAD model) to cut the inlet sheets.

Initially, I chose to make the mounting arm from Aluminum, however, it would experience very small strains, due to a low wind tunnel velocity (~ 10 m/s). So, I decided to make the mounting arm from Delrin (plastic), which has a smaller Young's Modulus than Aluminum and some 3D printed plastics. After making CAD in NX, I machined the mounting arm on a 3-Axis CNC mill.
