Subsonic Wind Tunnel

(May 2024 - Present)

Introduction

I have always wanted to make a subsonic wind tunnel due to two main reasons: (1) Visualize cool streamlines over various types of airfoils and (2) Measure lift and drag force over test articles and compare with NACA charts / CFD simulations.

Design

Minimizing build cost and ensuring ease of transportation were the two main design constraints. I started by choosing the inlet's contraction ratio, as this would drive the size of the test section and diffuser. I chose a 5:1 contraction ratio for maximum airspeed inside the test section, with inlet dimensions constrained by cheaper stock.

Inlet: Originally I designed a cone-shaped inlet, but then changed to a polynomial-curve shape for better flow characteristics. I cut out a 5th degree polynomial shape out of cardboard sheets with a laser cutter, and will epoxy them together to form the inlet contraction cone. Hinges will be used to remove the inlet and install test articles in the test section. To ensure laminar airflow, I plan to use 2-3 layers of window mesh to break down turbulent eddies in the intake air.

Test section: A sting arm protruding inside the test section will hold the test article. Load cells will be connected to the sting arm for measuring lift and drag forces.

Diffuser: By increasing the area, the diffuser recovers pressure losses from high-speed flow in the test section, and prevents backflow of air. It also contains the fan at the aft end, which pulls the intake air into the wind tunnel.

Manufacturing

I used a CNC Gantry router (at Purdueâ€™s BIDC ) to machine the inlet, test section, diffuser and secondary support structure from Plywood. Then, I assembled the main sections using two part structural epoxy and wood screws. I cut out rectangles for the support structure on a table saw and screwed them to the bottom of the inlet, test section and diffuser.

Next, I screwed a wireframe mesh to the front of the inlet, for breaking down incoming turbulent air to ensure laminar airflow inside the test section. To attach the cardboard sheets for the contraction cone, I used woodglue and clamped the sheets during the curing process.

Mounting arm design, and build

The mounting arm can not only hold the test article in the test section, but also change its Angle of Attack using a servo. I also plan to etch strain gages on its surface to read lift and drag loads on the test article. Initially, I chose to make the mounting arm from Aluminum, however, it would experience very small strains, due to a small load from a slow test section velocity (~ 10 m/s). So, I decided to make the mounting arm from Delrin (plastic), which has a smaller Young's Modulus than Aluminum and some 3D printed plastics. After making CAD in NX, I machined the mounting arm on a 3-Axis CNC mill.
