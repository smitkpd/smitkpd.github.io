Flutter Analysis

During initial design of my rocket team's fins, I investigated flutter to prevent structural failure due to resonance during flight. I started by referencing NACA TN 4197 and NACA Report 685, to understand underlying factors leading to flutter. I wanted to design a sandwich composite structure (to reduce mass), hence the analysis was more challenging compared to that for a linear, homogenous material (such as aluminum fins). Following was my approach:-

  • • Create FEM (Finite Element Model) of the composite fin in ANSYS using Composite PrepPost (ACP)

  • • Obtain natural frequencies and mode shapes through a Modal analysis (without applying external loads)

  • • Using the Strouhal number correlation (which relates velocity to an oscillating flowâ€™s frequency) -> adjust natural frequencies through stiffness changes (by changing carbon fiber ply count and orientation)

After consulting more experienced team members and engineers in industry, I learned that simplifying the analysis by using a representative beam model for the fin (with similar material properties) and correlating natural frequencies to a PSD (Power Spectral Density) curve would be a better way to do this. Although, I ended up changing to aluminum fins in the detailed design phase (due to greater mass savings in other parts of lower airframe), I gained a sense of how to simplify the flutter problem to not fall into the trap of analysis paralysis.

Fin Double-Wedge Airfoil analysis

To determine the optimal double-wedge airfoil shape for my rocket team's fins, I wrote a script to calculate drag across a supersonic double-wedge airfoil. I used the compressible flow equations for oblique and expansion shocks, to calculate the pressure ratio at each station. Drag can be derived from these pressure ratios. Additionally, I modeled a 2D double-wedge airfoil in CFD using ANSYS FLUENT to determine the drag coefficient, and validate with hand calculations.

FEA Mesh Convergence

For the FEM of upper airframe struts, I improved the mesh and bolt contact modeling, as well as conducted a mesh convergence study. Sharp transitions of tetrahedral mesh elements at the bolt holes led to unrealistically high stresses, giving a Safety Margin less than zero. To resolve this, I refined the mesh around bolt holes using a Sphere of Influence control, used non-linear material properties, and experimented with different bolt diameters and strut flange thicknesses.
