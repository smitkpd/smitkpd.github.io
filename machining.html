Parts for Purdue Space Program's Liquid Rocket

(Jan 2024 - Present)

Introduction

In Feb 2024, I became a Manufacturing Teaching Assistant at Purdue's BIDC. In this role, I consult students on CAM and operating HAAS 3 and 5-Axis CNC mills and lathes for manufacturing parts. This page describes the challenges and lessons learned from making parts for Purdue Space Program's Liquid Rocket team.

Raceway Elbow Connector

I created CAM and CNC machined a raceway elbow connector on a 5-Axis CNC mill. This part bolts to the lower tank bulkhead and houses one end of the raceway. A challenging part of the machining process was determining the approach to clamping the part, since only one side had flat surfaces on opposite sides. I chose to machine the portion with the flat faces first (to use them as clamping surfaces later). However, I realized that milling out the slot would cause the part to deform under the clamping force of the vice. So, the slot was machined in the very end.

For the angled bolt holes, I first bored out holes with the stock oriented at an angle to the endmill, then drilled the holes. This was to prevent the drill tip from deflecting/getting damaged, as the drill tip wouldn't be perpendicular to the stock. Moreover, I had to get creative with making sketches in CAD, to select specific contours for generating toolpaths in CAM. Lastly, upon feedback from a teammate, I designed an Aluminum support structure that would be clamped in the vice, for milling out a slot.

Launch Lugs

This was the first part where I operated a HAAS 3-Axis CNC mill entirely independently after getting trained. I machined the launch lugs in two operations, one half at a time, due to tool length limitations. For my first attempt, I probed off the stock (unreliable reference surface), leading to misalignment between the two halves. On my second attempt, I aligned the part with the vice's edge and probed off the vice edge - which singificantly reduced feature misalignment. Major takeaway here was to always probe off a machined or precision surface for aligning features between operations.

Valve Actuator Side Panels

These are side panels that are part of a larger assembly for a valve actuator. The hole in the middle required a locational interference fit with a bearing sleeve. To achieve this tolerance, I first bored out majority of the hole and then ran multiple contour passes. Between each contour pass, I measured the hole's inner diameter with a precision micrometer and adjusted the tool diameter offset in machine settings.

Additionally, for facing the stock on the bottom, I had to clamp the part in Kurt Vice. Since the final part's thickness was small, it bowed up easily upon applying clamping force, thus I was unable to seat the parallels on both sides. I learned that its only important to seat the parallel at the fixed jaw, and that reducing the clamping force allowed the part to sit flush with this parallel. Due to low clamping force, I reduced the axial depth of cut of the milling operation to reduce cutting forces, thus lower the chances of the part ripping off the vice.

Beginner Parts

(Aug 2022 - May 2023)

Titan 9M

This is a part I made to complete training for >Manufacturing Teaching Assistant at BIDC. I learned how to machine curved surfaces with a ball endmill, making counterbores and how to improve surface finish by reducing tool's radial depth of cut. I was quite happy with the final result!

Steel screw

As part of the CNC Lathe training, I made a steel screw. This involved facing and roughing the outside profile, grooving the slots, and threading. I learned how to secure the stock in a three jaw chuck in the lathe, set up tools and probe them.
