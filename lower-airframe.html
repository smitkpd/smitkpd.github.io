Purdue Space Program: Lower Airframe Responsible Engineer

(Jan - July 2023)

Introduction

As the Responsible Engineer, I owned the design, analysis and integration of the Lower Airframe in the Structures Team. I modeled the assembly in NX CAD, evaluated stress and buckling through hand calculations in MATLAB, prepared FEA in Ansys to evaluate stress concentrations for design optimization and created component engineering drawings with GD&T to convey functional intent for machining. Additionally, I managed and guided a team of 2-3 engineers to CAD components and prepare stress hand calculations.

Design

I navigated layout files in CAD (Siemens NX) to model components and identified dimensional constraints imposed by other assemblies such as propellant tank bulkheads and the engine. For the strut (vertical structural member), I chose an assymmetric T cross-section for the strut to allow for airframe panel mounting on both sides, and fin attachment to the web. For fin design, I optimized dimensions (root and tip chord, thickness, span, etc) for stability margin off-the-rail using RASAero (open source 6DOF simulator), and critical flutter velocity (for which, I used a safety factor of 1.5x Max Mach).

Analysis

I determined safety factors for max stress (compressive and bending), flexural and torsional buckling through hand calculations. I optimized the strut's cross-sectional dimensions for reducing mass, under Safety Factor constraints (1.5 for axial stress, 2.0 for bending stress and 5.0 for buckling).

Next, I created a FEM (Finite Element Model) of the struts in Ansys to determine the Von Mises Stress distribution and Buckling safety factors. I modeled the bolts as cylinders to reduce the simulation's computational cost. I conducted a mesh convergence study to ensure FEA results were not biased on mesh density, and validated results with hand calculations.

Integration

Since the lower airframe houses fluid systems (tubing, valves, Quick Disconnect ports, etc) and avionics equipment, I worked closely with responsible engineers on Fluid Systems and Avionics to allow for ease of access and handling. For example, I ensured the strut thickness was optimized for wrench clearances (for plumbing work), and minimal edge distance for avionics mounting bolt holes for mass savings. I also communicated with responsible engineers for tank bulkheads and the injector to optimize hole spacing for bolted connections and iterated the design for smooth integration.

Moreover, I created engineering drawings of the struts and fins, applying GD&T principles. I applied tighter tolerances on strut tab straightness and fin surface flatness to meet fin misalignment requirements for the rocket's max allowable roll rate. I also used material modifiers (specifically, MMC) for clearance holes at interfaces (strut to tank bulkhead and strut to fin) to allow for bonus tolerancing. I loved learning about how tolerancing and datum planes can be leveraged to convey the part's functional intent for machining!

Manufacturing

During early stages of design, I accounted for endmill tool radius to mininmize spacing between the fin tab and panel mounting holes on the struts. For the fins, after making preliminary CAM (Computer Aided Manufacturing) in Fusion 360, I realized that having the leading and trailing edges protrude from the fin tab would be better for the chamfer tool to clear the tip, as opposed to those edges being within the fin tab boundary.
