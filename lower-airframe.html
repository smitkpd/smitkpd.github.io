Purdue Space Program: Lower Airframe Responsible Engineer

(Jan - July 2023)

Introduction

As the Responsible Engineer, I owned the design, analysis and integration of the Lower Airframe structure. I managed a team of 2-3 engineers to CAD components and prepare stress hand calculations. Additionally, I presented the design and analysis to more experienced members and engineers from industry in a design review.

Design

For the struts (vertical structural members), I chose a T-cross section to allow for fin and airframe panel attachment, as well as its high bending moment of inertia. To size the cross-section, I performed hand calculations for stress and buckling safety factors. Next, I modeled the assembly in CAD (Siemens NX) using layout files. For preliminary design of fins, I optimized dimensions (root and tip chord, thickness, span, etc) for stability margin using RASAero (open source 6DOF simulator), and critical flutter velocity (using hand calculations per NACA TN 4197).

Analysis

I created a FEM (Finite Element Model) of the struts in Ansys to analyze stress concentrations at bolt holes and strut flanges. A challenge here was making the mesh finer to resolve stress artifacts, while ensuring the simulation runs resonably quickly. I experimented with various meshing controls (inflation layers, local element sizing, etc.) to find an optimal combination. I iterated bolt hole diameters and strut flange thicknesses to optimize for mass, while meeting required Safety Margins. Additionally, I conducted a mesh convergence study to ensure FEA results were not biased on mesh density.

Integration

I frequently communicated with Responsible Engineers on Fluid Systems and Avionics to account for clearances for fluid systems and mounting of avionics boards. For example, I optimized the strut's thickness based on minimal edge distance for bolt holes for avionics boards, resulting in mass savings. Further, I performed hand calculations for bolted joint design of strut to tank bulkhead and strut to injector interfaces.

Moreover, I created engineering drawings of the struts and fins with GD&T to convey tolerances and the functional intent for manufacturing. Upon feedback from peers, I implemented ordinate dimensions for hole positions and detailed views to improve the readibility of my drawings.

Manufacturing

Machining the fin consisted of 2 operations, as the cross-section was diamond/wedge shaped (i.e leading and trailing edges at opposite angle on other side of fin). To determine an optimal manufacturing strategy for the fins, I CNC machined 3 subscale fins to test different fixturing and probing approaches. Following are some of my learnings from this test campaign:-

  • • Initially, I machined slots in the 1st operation to use as reference for locating the stock in the 2nd operation. I used a long tool holder for making the slots, which led to tool vibrations and deviation in the slot geometry -> affecting the probing in 2nd op.

  • • Using ID expansion pins that thread into a sacrificial plate below the stock provide great positional and angular locating ability for aligning features on the fin's two sides. Additionally, they ensure repeatibility because of probing the sacrificial plate only once to machine 3 fins in one go.

  • • Stock in the 2nd op. did not have the same rigidity as the 1st op, due to material removed in 1st op. Thus, choosing a smaller tool diameter reduced cutting forces and deflection of stock -> resulting in better surface finish. Additionally, reducing the tool's axial depth of cut led to smooth surface finish of the leading and trailing edges.
