Purdue Space Program: Lower Airframe Responsible Engineer

(Jan - July 2023)

Introduction

As the Responsible Engineer, I owned the design, analysis and integration of the Lower Airframe in the Structures Team. I managed and guided a team of 2-3 engineers to CAD components and prepare stress hand calculations. Additionally, this position pushed me to communicate design and functional intent of parts with other sub-teams (Fluids and Avionics), and iterate design for smooth integration.

Design

I chose an assymmetric T-cross-section for the strut (vertical structural member) to allow for airframe panel mounting on both sides, and fin attachment to the web. For fin design, I optimized dimensions (root and tip chord, thickness, span, etc) for stability margin off-the-rail using RASAero (open source 6DOF simulator), and critical flutter velocity (for which, I used a safety factor of 1.5x Max Mach). The strut length was driven by fin root chord and plumbing length of Fluid Systems in lower airframe.

Analysis

I determined safety factors for max stress (compressive + bending), flexural and torsional buckling through hand calculations. I optimized the strut's cross-sectional dimensions for reducing mass, under Safety Factor constraints (1.5 for axial stress, 2.0 for bending stress and 5.0 for buckling).

Next, I created a FEM (Finite Element Model) of the struts in ANSYS to determine the Von Mises Stress distribution and Buckling safety factors. The bolts were modeled as cylinders to reduce the simulation's computational cost. I conducted a mesh convergence study to ensure FEA results were not biased to mesh density, and validated results with hand calculations.

Integration

Since the lower airframe houses fluid systems (tubing, valves, Quick Disconnect ports, etc) and avionics equipment, I worked closely with responsible engineers on these teams to allow for ease of access and handling. For example, I ensured the strut thickness was optimized for wrench clearances (for plumbing work), and minimal edge distance for avionics mounting bolt holes for mass savings.

Moreover, I created engineering drawings of the struts and fins, applying GD&T principles. Leveraging datum planes, I applied tighter tolerances on strut tab straightness and fin surface flatness to meet fin misalignment requirements for the rocket's maximum roll rate. I also used material modifiers (specifically, MMC) for clearance holes at interfaces (strut to tank bulkhead and strut to fin) to allow for bonus tolerancing. I loved learning about how tolerancing and datum planes can be leveraged to convey the part's functional intent to machinists!

Manufacturing

Embracing Design For Manufacturing, I accounted for endmill tool radius when minimizing space between the fin tab and panel mounting holes on the struts. For the fins, after making preliminary CAM (Computer Aided Manufacturing) in Fusion 360, I realized that having the leading and trailing edges protrude from the fin tab would be better for the chamfer tool to clear the tip, as opposed to those edges being within the fin tab boundary.
