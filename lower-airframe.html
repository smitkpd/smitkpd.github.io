Purdue Space Program: Lower Airframe Responsible Engineer

(Jan - July 2023)

Introduction

As the Responsible Engineer, I owned the design, analysis and integration of the Lower Airframe in the Structures Team. I modeled the assembly in NX CAD, evaluated stress and buckling through hand calculations in MATLAB, prepared FEA in Ansys to evaluate stress concentrations for design optimization and created component engineering drawings with GD&T to convey functional intent for manufacturing. Additionally, I managed and guided a team of 2-3 engineers to CAD components and prepare stress hand calculations.

Design

I navigated layout files in CAD (Siemens NX) to model components and identified dimensional constraints imposed by other assemblies such as propellant tank bulkheads and the engine. For the strut (vertical structural member), I chose an assymmetric T cross-section to allow for airframe panel mounting on both sides, and fin attachment to the web. For fin design, I optimized dimensions (root and tip chord, thickness, span, etc) for stability margin off-the-rail using RASAero (open source 6DOF simulator), and critical flutter velocity.

Analysis

I computed safety factors for maximum stress, flexural and torsional buckling through hand calculations. Then, I optimized the strut's cross-section for reduced mass.

Next, I created a FEM (Finite Element Model) of the struts in Ansys to determine the Von Mises Stress distribution and Eigenvalue Buckling safety factors. I conducted a mesh convergence study to ensure FEA results were not biased on mesh density, and validated results with hand calculations.

Integration

Since the lower airframe houses fluid systems (tubing, valves, Quick Disconnect ports, etc) and avionics equipment, I worked closely with responsible engineers on Fluid Systems and Avionics to allow for ease of access and handling. For example, I ensured the strut thickness was optimized for wrench clearances (for plumbing work), and minimal edge distance for avionics mounting bolt holes for mass savings. I also communicated with responsible engineers for tank bulkheads and the injector to optimize hole spacing for bolted connections and iterated the design for smooth integration.

Moreover, I created engineering drawings of the struts and fins, applying GD&T principles. I applied tighter tolerances on strut tab straightness and fin surface flatness to meet fin misalignment requirements for the rocket's max allowable roll rate. I also used material modifiers (specifically, MMC) for clearance holes at interfaces (strut to tank bulkhead and strut to fin) to allow for bonus tolerancing. I loved learning about how tolerancing and datum planes can be leveraged to convey the part's functional intent for machining!

Manufacturing

In the early stages of design, I accounted for endmill tool radius to mininmize spacing between the fin tab and panel mounting holes on the struts. To determine an optimal manufacturing strategy for the fins, I CNC machined 3 subscale fins to test different fixturing and probing approaches:-

  • • Subscale fin #1: Machined slots on the first operation, then flipped the stock and probed these slots to align the fin's front and back sides. Didn't achieve feature alignment, because of using a long 1/8" tool, which led to tool vibrations and deviation in slot geometry (Fig. 1) - affecting the probing operation. Bad surface finish on one face, because of using a large diameter face mill for second operation - where stock was not well supported (Fig. 2)

  • • Subscale fin #2: Dial indicated the slots to within 0.1 - 0.3 thousandth's of an inch, however, misalignment between front and back sides was still present. This might have between due to probe tip not contacting the same spot on the slot as first operation - could have been fixed in CAM. Validated no stock deflections when machining the leading and trailing edges with ball endmill, due to them being a tapered, thin edge. (Fig. 3)

  • • Subscale fin #3: Implemented ID expansion pins, which thread into sacrificial plate, and clamp the stock by expanding. Learned how to hit a tight tolerance for hole inner diameter by adjusting tool diameter offsets in machine, to provide a locational clearance fit for ID expansions pin collar. Achieved good feature alignment and only had to probe the sacrificial plate (fixed reference) once - great repeatibility for machining 3 flight scale fins in one go.
