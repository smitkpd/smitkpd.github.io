Subsonic Wind Tunnel

(May - July 2023)

Introduction

I have always wanted to make a subsonic wind tunnel due to two main reasons: (1) Visualize cool streamlines over various types of airfoils and (2) Measure lift and drag force over test articles and compare with NACA charts / CFD simulations.

Design

Minimizing build cost and ensuring ease of transportation were the two main design constraints. I started by choosing the inlet's contraction ratio, as this would drive the size of the test section and diffuser. I chose a 5:1 contraction ratio for maximum airspeed inside the test section, with inlet dimensions constrained by cheaper stock.

Inlet: The main challenge here is to construct the polynomial-shaped inlet geometry. To do this, I plan to create box-frame structural supports, (Figure X) then epoxy flexible cardboard sheets to them. Hinges will be used to allow for installation of test articles in the test section. To ensure laminar airflow, I plan to use 2-3 layers of window mesh to break down turbulent eddies in the intake air.

Test section: A sting arm protruding inside the test section will hold the test article. Load cells will be connected to the sting arm for measuring lift and drag forces.

Diffuser: This section recovers pressure losses from the high-speed flow in the test section, preventing backflow of air. It also contains the fan at the aft end, which pulls the intake air into the wind tunnel.

Electronics: Two load cells, for lift and drag measurements, are connected to an Arduino board. I calibrated the load cells with known weights.

Manufacturing

The inlet, test section and diffuser section are made from 0.5” thick plywood. I used a CNC Gantry router (at Purdue’s Bechtel Innovation Design Center ) epoxy and wood screws. I plan to complete manufacturing by end of December 2023 (after my Fall internship).
