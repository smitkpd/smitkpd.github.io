Micro Gas Turbine Research Project

(May - July 2023)

Introduction

In Summer 2023, I conducted undergraduate research, as part of Purdue SURF. I designed the electronics setup of and integrated 3-axis accelerometers for a Micro Gas Turbine engine to measure vibrations during testing. Additionally, I machined a shaft, compressor cap and engine mounting plate on CNC mills and lathes. I also procured testing hardware (bolts, mounting brackets, thermocouples, etc) and played an active role in engine assembly.

Vibration measurement

Vibration environment for aircraft engines has been extensively analyzed, however, not much literature exists for micro gas turbine (MGT) engines. For this reason, I chose to characterize how resonant frequencies change with air-fuel equivalence ratio. I procured 3-Axis accelerometers, soldered and connected them to an Arduino for reading acceleration values. I also prepared a MATLAB script to post-process the data and identify resonant frequencies using FFT (Fast Fourier Transform).

Although, our group couldn’t conduct testing with fuel (due to issues related to fit and tolerancing of components), I validated data collection from the accelerometers during initial engine tests.

Machining

Shaft: I machined the engine shaft with a peer mentor at Purdue’s BIDC. The tight diameter tolerance and square flat profile (Figure X) made this one of my biggest machining challenges. I had to repeatedly measure the shaft diameter and adjust the CAM (Computer Aided Machining) program to achieve the desired diameter. Moreover, I carried out multiple lathe operations to machine the shaft shoulders, as a high aspect ratio (length / diameter) stock would vibrate during machining. I used a 2-Axis CNC Mill to machine the flat surfaces, with an end mill.

Compressor Cap: This component prevents axial movement of the turbine during operation. I machined the base profile on a CNC lathe using cylindrical stock. In my first attempt of machining the hex features (for wrench grip during assembly), I had excess stock – which made the part very unstable on vice grips in a CNC Mill. For my second attempt, I used smaller stock and secured it in a chuck on a 5-Axis CNC Mill. I learned that the latter was significantly faster.
