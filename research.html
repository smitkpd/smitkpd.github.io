Micro Gas Turbine Research Project

(May - July 2023)

In Summer 2023, I conducted undergraduate research, as part of Purdue SURF at Purdue Zucrow Propulsion Labs.

Vibration measurement

Vibration environment for aircraft engines has been extensively analyzed, however, not much literature exists for micro gas turbine engines. For this reason, I chose to characterize how resonant frequencies change with air-fuel equivalence ratio. I procured 3-Axis accelerometers, soldered and connected them to an Arduino for reading acceleration values. I also prepared a MATLAB script to identify resonant frequencies through FFT (Fast Fourier Transform) analysis.

Although, our group couldn’t conduct testing with fuel (due to issues regarding fit and tolerancing of components), I validated data output from the accelerometers during initial engine tests. I also learned that enforcing tolerances based on the desired fit is crucial for parts like a shaft, where tight clearances are involved between shaft and load (bearings, turbine, compressor, etc.)

Machining

Shaft: I machined the engine shaft with a peer mentor at Purdue’s BIDC. The tight diameter tolerance and square flat profile made this one of my biggest machining challenges. I had to repeatedly measure the shaft diameter and adjust CAM (Computer Aided Machining) to achieve the desired diameter. Moreover, I carried out multiple lathe operations to machine the shaft shoulders, as a high aspect ratio (length / diameter) stock could impact cylindricity through vibrations during machining. I used a 2-Axis CNC Mill to machine the flat surfaces, with an end mill.

Machining

Compressor Cap: This component prevents axial movement of the turbine during operation. I machined the base profile on a CNC lathe using cylindrical stock. In my first attempt of machining the hex features (for wrench grip during assembly), I had excess stock – which made the part very unstable on vice grips in a CNC Mill. For my second attempt, I used smaller stock and secured it in a chuck on a 5-Axis CNC Mill. I learned that the latter was significantly faster.
